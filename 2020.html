Winter is My Favorite Season
‍
The weeknight was like any other weeknight in February — chilly, unassuming, dark. You had invited me to study with you, and despite my hatred for cold weather and leaving my cozy sheets, I managed to make that arduous three-block trek for you, which is something I would not have done for anybody else. For whatever reason, my heart just wasn’t in the right place, and I ended up leaving. To my surprise, you came after me, which only intensified that warm feeling that had been growing in my chest since late November. Come to think of it, it seems that that feeling was the only thing that made the New York winter bearable that year. Had you come a second later, I think I would’ve frozen over.

Though that weeknight seemed just like any other, we ended up in a car, headed down to the Brooklyn Bridge on a bout of your spontaneity. I don’t remember what was shared during the ride there, or if it was awkward at all. I just remember that that feeling I had been feeling all winter would not go away. It was gnawing at me. I also remember that I was so happy to be with you. Maybe this is because what came afterwards was the singular moment that finally aligned my path in a direction that was the utmost best to be taken.

When we got out of the car, we were in Brooklyn, facing the Brooklyn Bridge and Manhattan. In our range of sight was a restaurant, past its closing time for a couple of hours now, decorated with beaming, yellow lights. It felt like Christmas and magic and Paris, though those things could not truly compare to the experience that laid itself before me.

I remember being cold, but I remember that the cold was cut through by your arms wrapping around me. I remember the smell of your cologne, Cardamom & Mimosa, a scent to this day that I am continuously, feverishly infatuated with. (What is it about that scent that suits you so well?) I remember walking along the riverside until we found a bench, where we sat in a cold that didn’t seem so cold anymore.

I remember that you cut to the chase, which I’m sure took a decent amount of courage, and you asked me the question of the ages: “What are we?” The question, if phrased, “What do you want us to be?” would’ve been so easy for me to answer. What do I want us to be? I want us to be in love, to walk around Central Park like you always talked about; to cuddle up on your warm mattress, though it was on the floor, and watch films to our hearts content; to run away with you to Europe or something and forget everything else. But instead, you asked, “What are we?”, and that was a bit more difficult to answer for the sole reason that I did not know where you stood. The second that I learned that your heart sang for me like mine did for you, my hatred for New York in the winter thawed away.

You see, the thing is, my winter had been hard. It was a period of emptiness, self-loathing, and insecurity. Not only did I hate winters because they were freezing, but as someone who took the California sun for granted, I wished so much to go back to something more familiar and comfortable. But you were new. You were new and different, and you represented absolutely everything that I needed, that I was looking for. And the best part was, you were right in front of me, smiling as if you were the last pure source of warmth in this universe.

And suddenly, I could see the beauty in the trees, though they were laid bare by winter’s bite, and I could see the uniqueness of every snowflake caught in the cold breeze. Ever since then, I’ve warmed up to Christmas music, wearing an absurd amount of layers to stay warm, and the magic that surrounds the colder months. I began to hate the cold walks a bit less, especially if you were at the end of those ventures. I came to crave those colder months because it meant you stayed inside more often with me, because it would be nothing but you and I and the snow outside. You made the winters rosy.
